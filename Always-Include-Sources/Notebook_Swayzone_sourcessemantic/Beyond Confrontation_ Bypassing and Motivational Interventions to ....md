This chapter brings together an emerging class of interventions that may be used to fight false

information in unconventional ways. We first review traditional methods of confronting

misconceptions along with the disadvantages of these approaches. We then discuss research on

bypassing, a process in which beliefs are introduced or activated to counter the evaluative

implications of prior false beliefs. This method, which avoids confronting the false information

and is focused on attitude change, is discussed along with defensive confidence approaches to

reduce selective exposure to information, self-affirmation, paradoxical thinking, paradoxical

prescriptions, and motivational interviewing.

#### Beyond Confrontation:

Bypassing and Motivational Interventions to Curb the Impact of False Beliefs

For nearly two decades, the World Economic Forum has released the Global Risks

Report—a publication that summarizes what a panel of experts regards as the most salient threats

to the world over the next decade. The 2024 edition of this report ranked false information as the

top global risk, citing concerns regarding the use of artificial intelligence and other evolving

technologies in the spread of false information and its potential to deepen polarization and

disrupt elections. Similarly, a report by Pew found that 50% of Americans regarded false

information as a problem that needs to be fixed, a value that placed it above violent crime,

racism, and climate change. Although the exact way emerging technologies will impact the

spread and consequences of false information remains uncertain (Adams et al., 2023; Albarracín

et al., 2024; Altay et al., 2023; Lewandoswky et al., 2024), people are entitled to the truth.

Independent of the specific motivation, then, the study of how to best curb the uptake of false

information is critical to advancing institutional trustworthiness and democracy in the US and

A great deal of attention has been devoted to best practices for combatting false

information. The provision of a correction in which a misconception is flagged and refuted has

emerged as the most popular method for attenuating the impact of false information, with

research attesting to its value (Chan et al., 2017; Ecker et al., 2022; Walter & Murphy, 2018).

However, the efficacy of this confrontational approach may be limited by the arousal of

discomfort in the minds of recipients (Trevors, 2019; Wittenberg & Berinsky, 2020). The current

chapter explores an emerging class of interventions that bypass the false information along with

others that reduce defense motivation in unique ways. It also explores interventions to reduce

exposure to false beliefs, a promising avenue in the battle against information deception. Some

Traditional Confrontational Methods

for Responding to False Information

Most research on ways of curbing misinformation has examined the efficacy of

interventions that are either pre-emptive (i.e., before encountering false information) or reactive

in nature (i.e., after encountering false information; see Martel & Rand, 2023, for a review of

interventions that label information as false during exposure). Pre-emptive interventions seek to

prepare people to recognize and resist misinformation before they ever encounter it (Ecker et al.,

2022). Simpler pre-emptive techniques include warning people about the effects of false

information (Ecker et al., 2010; Karanian et al., 2008), with the most popular one involving

inoculation. Inoculation has its roots in the persuasion literature (Mcguire & Papageorgis, 1961;

Papageorgis & Mcguire, 1961), and has two primary elements. First, a motivation to defend

oneself from the manipulation must be aroused. Second, people must be exposed to information

that gives them the tools to resist manipulation, sometimes in the form of a weakened attack or

an example of the manipulation technique (van der Linden, 2022). Numerous experiments have

demonstrated that inoculation can successfully boost resistance to false information

(Lewandowsky & van der Linden, 2021; van der Linden, 2022).

As false information is often difficult to anticipate, however, most efforts to curb it

involve the provision of a correction. Corrections can focus on providing a fact-based refutation,

undermining trust in the information source, and/or pointing to logical fallacies in the claims

(Ecker et al., 2022). For example, if a person receives false information arguing that vaccinated

people contract COVID-19 more often than unvaccinated ones, a correction may point to the

false evidentiary basis of the claim (i.e., highlighting the misunderstanding of base rates) and

explain the current scientific understanding of the matter. Beyond the specific strategy employed,

research has examined matters related to the timing of a correction (Ahn et al., 2023; Brashier et

al., 2021), its structure (Connor Desai & Reimers, 2023), as well as its interaction with social,

cognitive, and social factors (Butler et al., 2024; Kessler & Bachmann, 2022; Sanderson et al.,

2022; Tao et al., 2023; Traberg et al., 2024).

Sufficient research has accumulated to enable various meta-analytic estimates of the

efficacy of corrections of false information. For example, the “debunking effect”, which assesses

how much a correction removes the impact of the false information on beliefs and attitudes has

been shown to be large or moderate (ds = 1.14-1.33; Chan et al., 2017; and d = .75; Walter &

Murphy, 2018). However, corrections are far from perfect. On average, some of the false

information presented in news-type formats persists in almost every case. Corrections of

scientifically relevant false information do not have a significant debunking effect and

corrections in the context of politics are less effective than those in the context of health

information (Chan & Albarracín, 2023). Beyond the specific information domain, corrections are

On the Limits of Corrections

When the provision of a correction fails to eliminate the effect of the false information on

people’s subsequent judgments, there is said to be a continued influence effect of false

information (Johnson & Seifert, 1994). In the classic paradigm demonstrating the effect (Wilkes

& Leatherbarrow, 1988), participants read a series of news statements about a fire in a

warehouse. Participants in the correction condition initially heard about a potential cause for the

fire (i.e., cans of oil paint and a gas cylinder being present in the room where the fire started),

after which this information was corrected (e.g., the room where the fire started was empty).

When participants were later asked to make inferences about the cause of the fire, those in the

correction condition mentioned the false information significantly more often than participants

who never read it and as much as participants who never saw a correction (Johnson & Seifert,

1994; Seifert, 2014; Wilkes & Leatherbarrow, 1988). This was the case even though recipients of

the correction could recall the correction.

A variety of cognitive mechanisms have been proposed to explain the continued

influence of false information. One prominent account posits that, as people receive information,

they build mental models to understand events and the relation between them. When false

information is corrected without modifying the resulting gap in one’s mental model, people may

still rely on the false information, which then has a continued influence on their judgment

(Johnson & Seifert, 1994; Lewandowsky et al., 2012). Other accounts focus on failures in

memory and processing fluency (see Lewandowsky et al., 2012, for an expanded discussion of

the cognitive mechanisms behind the continued influence effect). These purely cognitive

mechanisms operate under the assumption that the recipient accepts the validity of the correction.

Some degree of motivated rejection of the correction also drives the continued influence

of false information. In one experiment using a variation of the paradigm described above, most

participants (61-75%) who could correctly recall a correction did not believe the correction and

adhered to the false information as much as those who never saw the correction (O’Rear &

Radvansky, 2020; see also Ecker & Antonio, 2021). People appear to reject corrections at least in

part because acknowledging them can arouse discomfort. In fact, people who read a correction

report significantly more discomfort than those who read a confirmation of the same information

and this discomfort predicts continued belief in false information (Susmann & Wegener, 2021).

The discomfort aroused by corrections may stem from several psychological processes.

For instance, people may experience cognitive dissonance—an uncomfortable state of arousal

(Cooper, 2007; Festinger, 1957)—when they read a correction to false information they believe.

Moreover, a correction that is perceived as dictating how to think or behave can trigger

reactance, defined as a state of resistance or anger experienced when a person feels that their

freedom is thwarted (J. Brehm, 1972; J. W. Brehm, 1966; Shoham-Salomon et al., 1989). Such

discomfort may not only render corrections ineffective but also lead people to become more

entrenched in their belief in false information. Although such “worldview backfire” effects are

not exceedingly common (Nyhan, 2021; Wood & Porter, 2019), they have been documented in

the literature (Lewandowsky et al., 2012; Wittenberg & Berinsky, 2020). In one early qualitative

study (Prasad et al., 2009), Republicans who falsely believed that Saddam Hussein was linked to

the 9/11 terror attacks underwent “challenge” interviews that presented conclusive refutations of

the false belief (e.g., George W. Bush denying a link between Saddam and 9/11). Surprisingly,

only one of 49 participants (2%) believed the correction, with the rest engaging in a variety of

compensatory processes to maintain their belief. Subsequent quantitative research also

documented the backfire effect in the context of false information about Iraqi possession of

weapons of mass destruction during the Bush presidency. Conservative participants who read a

correction to this false information were more likely to believe the false information relative to

those who did not read the correction (Nyhan & Reifler, 2010). More recent work has also

documented backfire effects in the domain of vaccination (Nyhan et al., 2014) and politics

(Reinero et al., 2024). Worldview backfire effects seem to involve the experience of discomfort

in response to the correction, as demonstrated by analyses in which negative emotions mediate

the relation between corrections and the backfire effect (Trevors et al., 2016). Moreover, this

experienced discomfort seems accompanied by active counterarguing processes in which people

resist corrections effortfully (see Trevors, 2019 and Wittenberg & Berinsky, 2020, for reviews).

Non-Confrontational Methods for Addressing False Information

The traditional correction paradigm is fundamentally confrontational in nature because it

must identify the false information and directly refute it. This direct confrontation appears to

sometimes arouse discomfort in the minds of recipients, rendering the corrections less potent,

and sometimes even having ironic effects. Given that the experience of discomfort appears to

play a role in the failure of corrections, avoiding the arousal of such discomfort is important.

Thankfully, research has examined avenues by which false information can be addressed in ways

that are less likely to arouse discomfort. Some of this research is new, whereas other is more

classic, including efforts to understand how to manage the threat of external information to our

self-views and self-value. We begin by discussing research that directly changes the information

available as a basis for beliefs and attitudes. We then turn to approaches that more directly

address motivated defense mechanisms.

Bypassing False Information

CURBING THE IMPACT OF FALSE BELEFS  9

An alternative to the standard correction of false information is referred to as bypassing

(Calabrese & Albarracín, 2023). The bypassing approach seeks to attenuate the impact of false

information on people’s attitudes and behavior by forgoing direct confrontation and instead

focusing on introducing or bolstering beliefs that have an opposite evaluative implication relative

to the false information. Bypassing false information is effective because it relies on the mental

organization of the representations of beliefs, attitudes, and behavioral intentions. Beliefs refer to

a probability judgment linking an object (e.g., person, place, thing) to an outcome or attribute

(Albarracín, 2021; Fishbein & Ajzen, 1975, 2010a; Wyer & Albarracín, 2005). Attitudes can be

conceptualized as evaluations of an object along a positive-negative dimension (Albarracín et al.,

2005; Fazio, 2007; Fishbein & Ajzen, 1975; Petty et al., 1997). Importantly, expectancy-value

models view attitudes as being a function of people’s beliefs regarding the attributes associated

with an object and their evaluation of each attribute (Fishbein, 1963; Fishbein & Ajzen, 1975).

Similarly, information integration theory conceptualizes attitudes as the outcome of processes in

which people integrate their beliefs about an object using cognitive algebra (Anderson, 1971,

1973). In turn, behavioral attitudes are conceptualized as being a key determinant of people’s

intentions to perform a behavior, which is the most proximate predictor of behavior (Fishbein &

Ajzen, 1975, 2010b).

With these theoretical models in mind, it becomes clear that direct confrontation of false

beliefs is only one process by which the downstream impact of those false beliefs on attitudes,

intentions, and behavior can be attenuated. An alternative way of combating the influence of

false information, then, is to change attitudes or intentions by inserting or highlighting beliefs

whose evaluative implications are opposite to those of the false information. For example,

imagine that a person has been exposed to false information promoting the idea that genetically

CURBING THE IMPACT OF FALSE BELEFS  10

modified (GM) foods are associated with negative health effects. Usually, such false information

would be addressed via a correction, even though the objective is the revision of the relevant

false belief to update associated attitudes and behavioral intentions. An intervention based on the

bypassing approach would avoid mentioning the false information at all, instead focusing on

either adding new beliefs or bolstering existing beliefs about GM foods with a positive

evaluative implication. Such bypassing beliefs are predicted to make attitudes related to the

consumption of GM foods more positive relative to those only exposed to false information.

Bypassing has been shown to effectively limit the impact of false information on people’s

attitudes and behavioral intentions. In one experiment (Calabrese & Albarracín, 2023),

participants first read an article communicating false information linking the consumption of GM

foods to allergic reactions. After being exposed to false information, participants were randomly

assigned to either (a) read an article highlighting the value of GM foods for combating world

hunger (bypassing condition), (b) read an article issuing a correction to refute the first article

(correction condition), or (c) not read an additional article (false information control condition).

Lastly, participants reported their belief in the false information, belief that GM foods help

alleviate hunger, attitudes toward policies that seek to restrict the use of GM foods, and

intentions to support such restrictive policies. Relative to the bypassing and control conditions,

those who read the correction reported less belief in the false information, as expected. The

bypassing and control conditions did not differ from each other in this regard. Moreover, there

was no significant difference in the bypassing belief (i.e., the belief that GM foods alleviate

world hunger), suggesting that this belief was already somewhat prevalent in the population

studied. More to the point, those in both the bypassing and correction conditions significantly

reduced support for policies aimed at restricting the use of GM foods and intentions to support

restrictive GM food policies relative to the false information control condition, with no

difference between the bypassing and correction conditions. That is, bypassing was as effective

as correction at attenuating the downstream influence of false information (Calabrese &

Albarracín, 2023).

Two additional experiments provided converging evidence for the effectiveness of

bypassing (Calabrese & Albarracín, 2023). Given that the belief that GM foods help alleviate

world hunger was already prevalent among participants, the second experiment largely replicated

the prior investigation, but sought to test whether bypassing false information introducing a new

belief—that GM foods help the bee population—could yield similar results. As expected, those

in the bypassing condition reported stronger belief that GM foods can help save the bee

population relative to those in the false information and control conditions. In addition, those in

the correction condition showed less belief in the false information about GM foods relative to

both the bypassing condition and the control condition, with those exposed to the bypassing

message showing less belief than control participants. Beyond these differences, the results of

the first experiment were replicated: relative to the control, bypassing and correction led to

significantly less positive attitudes toward restricting the use of GM foods and weaker intentions

to support such policies and no difference emerged between bypassing and correction (Calabrese

& Albarracín, 2023).

One additional experiment employed a false information article communicating more

serious health consequences of consuming GM foods and included a true control in which

participants were not exposed to false information at all (true control condition). Consistent with

the prior work, those in the correction condition reported less endorsement of the false

information relative to the bypassing and control conditions. In fact, endorsement of false

information in the correction condition was indistinguishable from the true control. Regarding

the bypassing belief, those in the bypassing condition endorsed it to a greater extent relative to

all other conditions. Lastly, bypassing and correction were once again successful at attenuating

the impact of false information on attitudes toward restrictive policies and intentions to support

restrictive policies relative to the false information control condition, yet both bypassing and

correction were not different from the true control condition (Calabrese & Albarracín, 2023).

Together, these three experiments provided strong support for the effectiveness of bypassing in

attenuating the downstream impact of false information in a manner similar to that of correction.

Subsequent research followed up on the seminal work described above by developing a

within-subjects paradigm in which false information is presented via briefer fictitious news

headlines crafted to mimic false information in the real world (Granados Samayoa & Albarracín,

2024). This new paradigm confers two benefits. First, within-subjects paradigms afford greater

statistical power (Greenwald, 1976). Second, the new paradigm focuses on communication via

brief headlines, which constitutes another important method by which people consume news.

The general paradigm proceeded as follows: on each trial, participants read an initial

headline constituting false information (“The chemical TSF causes anxiety”), followed by either

(a) a bypassing message (“The chemical TSF reduces the price of goods”), (b) a correction (“The

chemical TSF does not cause anxiety”), or (c) an unrelated control message (“City council

approves new bike lane”). Half of the false information headlines were negative (“The chemical

TSF causes anxiety”) and half were positive (“The food additive vernabol reduces the price of

food”). All participants completed twelve trials, equally split into bypassing, correction, and

control trials. The false information and bypassing statements were pre-tested to assess their

evaluative extremity and matched so that any discrepancy in evaluative extremity was

minimized. After reading a pair of headlines (the false information and either the bypassing,

correction, or control message), participants reported their attitudes toward policies seeking to

restrict the object in question and their intentions to support such a policy. Consistent with the

results of Calabrese and Albarracín (2023), the impact of false information headlines on people’s

attitudes toward restrictive policies and intentions to support those restrictive policies was

attenuated on bypassing and correction trials relative to control trials. In a departure from the

prior set of findings, however, bypassing was significantly more effective at shifting attitudes

toward restrictive policies and intentions to support restrictive policies relative to correction

(Granados Samayoa & Albarracín, 2024). Thus, in the context of news headlines, bypassing

enjoys an advantage over correction in terms of attenuating the downstream impact of false

information.

What cognitive processes might account for this advantage of bypassing over correction?

Subsequent research sought to shed light on this question by examining the degree to which

amount of thinking and processing goals moderated the advantage enjoyed by bypassing. First,

because negating a belief is a resource-intensive process, bypassing was hypothesized to surpass

correction when the motivation and/or ability to process information were relatively limited. In

contrast to this hypothesis, however, a well-powered experiment found that a manipulation of

motivation and ability to process the news headlines did not affect the effectiveness of bypassing

(Granados Samayoa & Albarracín, 2024).

Second, investigators focused on instilling different processing goals as people read news

headlines. As people process information, they form beliefs by linking objects to attributes (e.g.,

linking the chemical TSF to anxiety). However, people can go beyond the formation of beliefs

and use those beliefs to continually form an attitude toward the issue being considered (e.g., the

CURBING THE IMPACT OF FALSE BELEFS  14

chemical TSF is bad). These two processing goals may interact differently with bypassing

messages and corrections. When people are focused on only forming beliefs, they are likely to

store both the false information and the bypassing information or the correction in memory.

When asked to provide an evaluation of the focal object, participants will recall the relevant

information and render an evaluative judgment. In this situation, the recent bypassing

information—with its salient opposite evaluative implication—may play a larger role in the

construction of an attitude, and thus, enjoy an advantage over the correction. However, when

people form attitudes with the first information they receive, the false information will taint

people’s attitudes toward the focal object. Upon receiving a bypassing message or correction,

people in both instances will have to engage in cognitive change of the attitude, lending the

prediction that bypassing will not enjoy an advantage over correction.

To test these predictions about the impact of processing goals on bypassing and

correction, participants were randomly assigned either to only form beliefs or to form attitudes as

they read the news headlines. As predicted, bypassing was better at attenuating the impact of

false information when people were led to form beliefs—an advantage mirrored in a control

condition in which no explicit processing goals were instilled—but bypassing was as effective as

corrections when people were led to form attitudes (Granados Samayoa & Albarracín, 2024). In

sum, bypassing resembles correction in the context of reading news articles, and even more

effective in the context of news headlines. The advantage of bypassing documented in the latter

set of studies appears to be at least partly attributable to processing goals that led individuals to

primarily focus on forming beliefs.

Motivated Processes

Even though much of the research on the impact of false information addresses cognitive

processes, the fact that its correction arouses discomfort suggests that other mechanisms deserve

close attention. Therefore, in this section, we discuss the self and interpersonal implications of

attempts at changing somebody’s mind, along with ways of producing changes in these contexts.

We cover methods to reduce selective exposure to false information, including paradoxical

encouragement of defensive abilities and self-affirmation; paradoxical thinking; paradoxical

prescriptions; and motivational interviewing.

Paradoxically Encouraging Defensive Abilities. Encouraging people to defend

themselves from external attacks is one of the methods that paradoxically decreases information

avoidance once false information takes hold. While doubting one’s ability to resist persuasion

typically reduces exposure to the information (Albarracín & Mitchell, 2004; Byrne, 1961; Olson

& Zanna, 1982), instilling confidence can ironically encourage individuals to face it. Support for

this notion has come from work on both specific confidence and defensive confidence. With

respect to specific confidence, individuals who previously failed at solving a problem are more

likely to approach information that supports their incorrect answer than information that counters

it (Cannon, 1964). With respect to defensive confidence, people vary in their beliefs that they can

effectively counterargue information with which they disagree (Albarracín & Mitchell, 2004).

These perceptions arise from both temporary thoughts about having lost an argument, as well as

more stable personality traits measured with items like “No matter what I read or hear, I am

always capable of defending my feelings and opinions” and “During discussions of issues I care

about, I can successfully defend my ideas.”

Although research on defensive confidence has not been conducted with misinformation

specifically, past research has addressed it role in changing attitudes and behavior. In a series of

studies by Albarracín and Mitchell (2004), participants with greater defensive confidence were

more willing to read counterattitudinal information about abortion, euthanasia, and gun control.

They were also more likely to choose the counterattitudinal information after writing about a

time when they had successfully defended their attitudes. Thus, chronic and temporary sources

of defensive confidence increased exposure to information participants would otherwise avoid.

More importantly, participants who received the information they were avoiding ended up

changing their attitudes as well.

The defensive confidence notion has been used to increase exposure to a behavioral

change intervention in the HIV prevention domain (Albarracín et al., 2008). In a randomized

control trial that took please in Florida (United States), participants with high HIV risk were

invited to participate in counseling to reduce their vulnerability. The invitations involved a brief

explanation of the program, followed by one of two statements (a) “The point of the counseling

session is to provide you with the most current information, not to influence your opinion or

make you use condoms if you don’t want to” and (b) “Most of the people who participate in

counseling said that it improved their lives and they wished they had participated sooner.” A

control group did not receive either of these specific statements. As predicted, participants were

more likely to enroll in response to the first statement, which instilled defensive confidence, thus

empowering participants to approach information that challenged their past behavior.

Although defensive confidence has not been investigated with misinformation, future

research should investigate its applicability to false information. Often, people who endorse false

information live in environments where considerable motivation might be necessary to find

accurate information. In this context, a challenge to seek accurate information might be useful.

Furthermore, making people confident in other abilities can be effective as well. For example,

recipients of false information have been self-affirmed to rely on their memory, and this can be

sufficient to reduce reliance on false information within their environments (Szpitalak &

Polczyk, 2019).

Self-Affirmation to Reduce Avoidance. Other approaches to induce exposure to

information have been based on the notion that reinforcing the self promotes openness to

uncomfortable information. In this framework, individuals are motivated to think that the self is

good and culturally and socially appropriate (Aronson et al., 1999; D. A. K. Sherman et al.,

2000; D. K. Sherman & Cohen, 2002). Thus, when their sense of self is threatened, they engage

in defensive responses such as avoiding or counterarguing the threatening information or its

sources. However, certain adaptations allow individuals to face reality by reducing these

defensive responses. For example, validating the self by taking stock of other strengths acts as a

“self‐affirmation,” reducing the need to defend the self. As a result, after self-affirming, people

are more likely to face dissatisfying aspects of the self and maintain contact with threatening

information.

In the area of persuasion, research has examined responses to threatening health

information about breast cancer (D. A. K. Sherman et al., 2000). Women who either did or did

not drink coffee were presented with information that caffeine consumption increases fibrocystic

disease, which can in turn result in breast cancer. Participants in control conditions were not self-

affirmed and dismissed information suggesting that they were at risk, as suggested by classic

motivated reasoning findings (see Kunda, 1987). However, women who were self-affirmed by

completing a scale about their central values, including religious ones, accepted the threatening

risk information more. In sum, self-affirmation reduced the motivation to protect the self by

rejecting threatening information.

Self-affirmation has also been used to increase the impact of misinformation correction.

In research by Carnahan et al. (2018), corrections of false information were more effective when

people had previously completed a value questionnaire that self-affirmed them. Having found

forms of supporting their views of the self, participants had less need to self-affirm by defending

their false beliefs. As a result, they were more likely to abandon false beliefs.

Paradoxical Thinking or Reduction to Absurdity. Other interventions have used

reductio ad absurdum as a method to encourage individuals to spontaneously question their

beliefs (Bar-Tal et al., 2021). Some of this research was conducted by Swann and Graddol

(1988), who used extreme questions about sexism to dissuade participants of stereotypical

attitudes. For example, one can ask a person who endorses traditional gender stereotypes to

answer the question, “Why do you sympathize with the feelings of some men that women are

better kept barefoot and pregnant?”. In Swann and Graddol’s research, asking ten such leading

questions reduced gender stereotypes among participants who were most convinced of those

stereotypes at the beginning of the study.

Using the same principle, Hameiri et al. (2016) developed messages to depolarize beliefs

about the Israel-Palestine conflict. This research involved common beliefs among Israeli Jews,

which were presented with messages about the same beliefs in more extreme forms. For

example, as many at the time believed that the conflict had improved the Israeli military, an

experimental message read “Without it \[the conflict\] we wouldn’t have had the strongest army in

the world ... For the army, we probably need the conflict.” The paradoxical messages again

worked to encourage disagreement with the exaggerated beliefs, reducing support for the conflict

to a greater extent than control appeals. Moreover, the effects were most pronounced among

participants with right-wing attitudes, similar to the findings by Swann and Graddol (1988).

Other research has uncovered the mechanisms of paradoxical arguments to absurdity

(Hamieri et al., 2018). Participants were asked ten leading questions like the ones used by Swann

and Graddol’s (1988), although in reference to the Israel-Palestine conflict. For example,

participants would answer the question, “Why do you think that the real and only goal the

Palestinians have in mind is to annihilate us, in a manner that transcends their basic needs such

as food and health?”. Results indicated that participants who answered extreme questions in the

direction of their beliefs experienced (Hameiri et al., 2017) surprise, disagreed with the question,

and experienced less identity threat. All in all, each of these mechanisms made the absurd

questions more dissuasive, reducing false beliefs about a seemingly intractable conflict.

Paradoxical Prescriptions. The most famous paradoxical interventions involve attempts

to reduce a symptom or undesirable behavior by directly prescribing it (Seltzer, 1986; Shoham-

Salomon et al., 1989). A patient who suffers from insomnia might be asked to stay awake

voluntarily, and a person who worries may be told to continue to do so but in a controlled,

systematic fashion. According to Watzlawick, Beavin, and Jackson (1967), prescribing a

symptom or behavior can change it because it places the patient in the “be spontaneous” paradox.

In this “double bind,” the client complies with the therapist either if they do or do not change,

and either result can alter how the undesirable behavior is perceived.

One possible mechanism why paradoxical prescription works is akin to psychological

reactance, defined as a state of resistance or anger experienced when a person feels that their

freedom is thwarted (J. Brehm, 1972; J. W. Brehm, 1966; Shoham-Salomon et al., 1989). In

addition, however, Shoham-Salomon et al. (1989) have examined the role of self-efficacy. In

fact, self-efficacy can improve both when patients do and do not change. Individuals who change

may perceive that they are now in control of the symptom, whereas those who continue to

experience the symptom are “complying” with the therapist’s prescription and, in doing so, are at

least controlling some aspect of their behavior. As a result, the prescription could produce

improvements in self-efficacy and behavior in either group (Shoham-Salomon et al., 1989).

In two studies, Shoham-Salomon et al. (1989) tested the role of psychological reactance

and self-efficacy by either prescribing procrastination or teaching self-control to students.

According to the study hypotheses, participants with higher levels of trait reactance should

reduce procrastination in response to a paradoxical prescription, whereas those with lower levels

of reactance should improve in response to the self-control treatment. The paradoxical

interventions involved a therapist who told the patients that "although it might sound a bit

strange, it is very important that you try to bring your procrastination about deliberately."

Participants were then allowed to organize study materials but not study, concentrating “on

producing the procrastination." The self-control intervention taught clients to reduce

procrastination by controlling the situation, such as finding quiet contexts in which they could

study more effectively.

The studies from Shoham-Salomon et al. (1989) essentially showed that participants with

a reactance predisposition changed more in response to the paradoxical prescription even though

their self-efficacy increased only in the self-control condition. In contrast, patients without a

reactance predisposition changed more in response to the self-control intervention, even though

the paradoxical intervention led to greater self-efficacy.

Although these effects of paradoxical interventions are intriguing, a meta-analysis of

them showed variable impacts (d = .05 to 2.20; Shoham-Salomon & Rosenthal, 1987) and no

applications to belief change. However, a clinical domain where beliefs are central is Obsessive

Compulsive Disorder (OCD), where beliefs (i.e., obsessions) perceived as irrational pop to mind

without control. We identified one randomized controlled trial in which a paradoxical timetable

was used for ruminations and self-referential thoughts (Namazi & Anasseri, 2023). The

intervention involved scheduling times to think about one’s worries or experience a particular

type of thought, again introducing a prescription of the symptom. This study revealed significant

decreases in the activation of the undesirable symptoms, suggesting potential promise of this

approach in the case of belief. Whether these effects could work to curb misinformation,

however, needs to be investigated in the future.

Motivational Interviewing and Empathetic Refutational Interviewing. Another

general way to bring about change by circumventing resistance is to adopt an empathetic and

empowering stance. The roots of this approach can be traced back to a dissatisfaction of the

traditional paradigm of counseling individuals with substance use issues (Miller, 1983).

Motivational interviewing has been defined as “a particular way of talking with people about

change and growth to strengthen their own motivation and commitment” (Miller & Rollnick,

2023, p. 3). This way of bringing about change is rooted in principles of forming partnerships,

acceptance, compassion, and patient empowerment (Breckenridge et al., 2022; Miller &

Rollnick, 2023). Motivational interviewing, then, can be described as a guiding style of

communication in which an interviewer largely listens and provides advice only when

appropriate. This style of communication can be contrasted with a directing style in which the

interviewer tells the client what to do (Boness et al., 2022), which can lead to discomfort and

counterarguing on the part of the client. Importantly, motivational interviewing has been found

to be effective for bringing about change across many domains (Miller, 2023; Miller & Rollnick,

2023), including vaccination in which false information is prevalent (Abroms et al., 2024;

Boness et al., 2022; Breckenridge et al., 2022; Gabarda & Butterworth, 2021).

Much like bypassing and the paradoxical methods we just reviewed, motivational

interviewing represents a non-confrontational way of pursuing change. It can be used to dispel

false information in a less confrontational manner, using techniques like elicit-provide-elicit. In

this approach, an interviewer can inquire about a person’s understanding of a given concept like

vaccination (elicit), provide information that might correct any misconceptions in a respectful

manner (provide), and then seek feedback on the information provided (elicit; Boness et al.,

Most relevant to curbing false information is recent work examining the efficacy of

motivational interviewing principles using a technique called empathetic refutational

interviewing (Holford et al., 2024). Empathetic refutational interviewing consists of four steps:

(a) eliciting concerns from patients about the focal object (e.g., safety record of vaccines), (b)

affirming the patient’s concerns, (c) providing a correction of the false information that is

tailored to the patient’s concerns, and (d) providing facts that go beyond the correction (e.g.,

benefits of herd immunity). In that way, empathetic refutational interviewing incorporates

aspects of motivational interviewing (a and b), traditional corrections (c), and educational

campaigns (d). The initial empirical test of the value of empathetic refutational interviewing used

a paradigm in which participants read about or watched an interaction between a vaccine hesitant

individual and a medical professional who implements different aspects of empathetic

refutational interviewing (Holford et al., 2024). Across experiments, participants who were

somewhat vaccine hesitant themselves evaluated the interaction between the vaccine hesitant

citizen and the medical professional more positively when the medical professional delivered an

empathetic correction relative to a more traditional one. Moreover, participants were less

supportive of anti-vaccine arguments and reported less vaccine hesitancy when the interaction

they witnessed implemented all steps of empathetic refutational interviewing relative to a control

condition (Holford et al., 2024). Notably, however, these effects on support for anti-vaccine

arguments and vaccine hesitancy did not differ between the condition employing all empathetic

refutational interviewing steps and those employing only certain elements of it. Research on the

efficacy of empathetic refutational interviewing is in its infancy. However, given the artificial

and light nature of the manipulation, the ability to move support for anti-vaccine arguments and

vaccine hesitancy scores is impressive.

Conclusions

The spread of false information represents a problem worth fighting, independent of the

specific motivation for doing so. Much attention has been devoted to the study of the efficacy of

corrections. Although corrections tend to work well on average (Chan et al., 2017; Walter &

Murphy, 2018), their efficacy can be limited by a variety of factors, including the arousal of the

discomfort that accompanies direct confrontation of beliefs (Trevors, 2019; Wittenberg &

Berinsky, 2020). The current chapter reviewed emerging research on non-confrontational

methods for combatting misinformation. First, we reviewed empirical work testing the efficacy

of bypassing, the introduction or bolstering of alternative beliefs whose evaluative implication is

opposite to that of the false information, in attenuating the impact of false information on

people’s attitudes and intentions. Bypassing appears to be as effective as corrections in

combatting the influence of false information on people’s attitudes and intentions in the context

of long news articles. It is also more effective than corrections in the context of news headline

reading. The advantage of bypassing relative to correction appears to be driven by the processing

goals of the recipient, with a greater focus on belief formation conferring bypassing its benefit.

Second, we reviewed defensive confidence approaches, self-affirmation, paradoxical thinking,

CURBING THE IMPACT OF FALSE BELEFS  24

and paradoxical prescriptions, finishing with the use of motivational interviewing and the related

empathetic refutational interviewing in the context of false information.

These methods of bringing about change share a focus on circumventing the discomfort

that direct confrontation in a direct style can induce. However, they differ in their treatment of

the false information itself. Whereas bypassing forgoes mentioning false information at all,

motivational interviewing and empathetic refutational interviewing opt for adopting and

empathetic, empowering, and supportive stance that is meant to diffuse any discomfort and

counterarguing on the part of the client, yet still addresses the false information directly. It is fair

to characterize the state of the literatures covered here as relatively nascent. Indeed, most of the

evidence supporting the value of the non-confrontational methods consists of measures of

beliefs, attitudes, or intentions (e.g., Holford et al., 2024). When the evidence does concern

behavior (Albarracín et al., 2008), it has not been specifically applied to the correction of false

information. That said, the present represents an exciting time for the study of non-

confrontational methods of combating the effects of false information as interest in these

approaches appears to be rising and false information continues to multiply.
