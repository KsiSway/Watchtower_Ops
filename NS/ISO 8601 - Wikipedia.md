international standard

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International\_standard

covering the worldwide exchange and communication of date and

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time

-related data. It is maintained by the

International Organization for Standardization

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International\_Organization\_for\_Standardization

(ISO) and was first published in 1988, with updates in 1991, 2000, 2004, and 2019, and an amendment in 2022.

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-:0-1

The standard provides a well-defined, unambiguous method of representing calendar dates and times in worldwide communications, especially to avoid misinterpreting numeric dates and times when such data is transferred between

countries with different conventions

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Date\_and\_time\_representation\_by\_country

for writing numeric dates and times.

ISO 8601 applies to these representations and formats: dates, in the

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian\_calendar

calendar (including the

proleptic Gregorian

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proleptic\_Gregorian\_calendar

calendar); times, based on the

24-hour timekeeping system

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/24-hour\_clock

, with optional

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTC\_offset

time intervals

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time\_interval

, and combinations thereof.

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-scope-2

The standard does not assign specific meaning to any element of the dates/times represented: the meaning of any element depends on the context of its use. Dates and times represented cannot use words that do not have a specified numerical meaning within the standard (thus excluding

names of years

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese\_calendar\_correspondence\_table

Chinese calendar

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese\_calendar

), or that do not use

computer characters

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character\_(computing)

(excludes images or sounds).

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-scope-2

In representations that adhere to the ISO 8601 interchange standard, dates and times are arranged such that the greatest temporal term (typically a year) is placed at the left and each successively lesser term is placed to the right of the previous term. Representations must be written in a combination of

Arabic numerals

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic\_numerals

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-:1-3

and the specific computer characters (such as "‐", ":", "T", "W", "Z") that are assigned specific meanings within the standard; that is, such commonplace descriptors of dates (or parts of dates) as "January", "Thursday", or "New Year's Day" are not allowed in interchange representations within the standard.

http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISO\_8601&action=edit&section=1

The first edition of the ISO 8601 standard was published as

ISO 8601:1988

in June 1988. It unified and replaced a number of older ISO standards on various aspects of date and time notation

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-doc2004-4

Writing of calendar dates in all-numeric form

, published April 1976

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-ISO214-5

following ISO/R 2014 from 1971, originally introduced the hyphenated,

most-to-least-significant

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Significant\_figures

order notation for

Gregorian calendar

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian\_calendar

dates with day of the month as an international standard.

Numbering of weeks

, also from April 1976

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-ISO215-6

with ISO R 2015 from 1971, first specified the rules for determining the ordinal number of a calendar week in a year and a day within a week.

Representation of ordinal dates

, issued in January 1973,

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-ISO2711-7

introduced the notation for the day of the year, which effectively treats only the final, 366th day of leap years differently.

Representation of time of the day

, dated March 1975,

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-ISO3307-8

standardized 24-hour wall clock times and their notation, but without handling time zones.

Representation of time of the day

, published December 1978,

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-ISO4031-9

added numerical offsets from UTC (or GMT back then).

This first, unified edition was superseded by a second edition,

ISO 8601:2000

, in 2000, that by a revised, third edition,

ISO 8601:2004

, published on 1 December 2004, and that by

ISO 8601-1:2019

ISO 8601-2:2019

on 25 February 2019. All editions and parts of ISO 8601 and its predecessors were prepared by,

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-10

and are under the direct responsibility of,

ISO Technical Committee

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List\_of\_ISO\_technical\_committees

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-11

Issued in February 2019,

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-:1-3

the fourth revision of the standard, ISO 8601-1:2019, represents slightly updated contents of the previous ISO 8601:2004 standard,

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-12

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-:0-1

but is now the first of two parts. The other part, ISO 8601-2:2019, is entirely new. It defines various extensions such as uncertainties or parts of the

Extended Date/Time Format

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#EDTF

General principles

http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISO\_8601&action=edit&section=2

Date and time values are ordered from the largest to smallest unit of time: year, month (or week), day, hour, minute, second, and fraction of second. The

lexicographical order

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lexicographical\_order

of the representation thus corresponds to chronological order, except for date representations involving negative years or time offset. This allows dates to be naturally

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sorting

by, for example, file systems.

Each date and time value has a fixed number of digits that must be padded with

leading zeros

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leading\_zero

Representations can be done in one of two formats – a

basic format

with a minimal number of separators or an

extended format

with separators added to enhance human readability.

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-isofaqtnd-19

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-20

The standard notes that "The basic format should be avoided in

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plain\_text

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-21

The separator used between date values (year, month, week, and day) is the

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyphen

, while the

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colon\_(punctuation)

is used as the separator between time values (hours, minutes, and seconds). For example, the 6th day of the 1st month of the year 2009 may be written as "2009-01-06" in the extended format or as "20090106" in the basic format without ambiguity.

For reduced

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precision\_and\_accuracy

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-AccuracyVsPrecision-22

any number of values may be dropped from any of the date and time representations, but in the order from the least to the most significant. For example, "2004-05" is a valid ISO 8601 date, which indicates May (the fifth month) 2004. This format will never represent the 5th day of an unspecified month in 2004, nor will it represent a time-span extending from 2004 into 2005.

If necessary for a particular application, the standard supports the addition of a

decimal fraction

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal\_fractions

to the smallest time value in the representation.

http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISO\_8601&action=edit&section=3

The standard uses the

Gregorian calendar

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian\_calendar

, which "serves as an international standard for civil use".

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-23

ISO 8601 allows Gregorian dates from the introduction of the calendar on 15 October 1582. For earlier (pre-Gregorian) dates, the calendar may be extended before its introduction (the

proleptic Gregorian calendar

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proleptic\_Gregorian\_calendar

) by explicit agreement of the parties involved. Such proleptic dates may not be adjusted to reconcile them with Julian dates.

http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISO\_8601&action=edit&section=4

ISO 8601 prescribes, as a minimum, a four-digit year \[YYYY\] to avoid the

year 2000 problem

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Year\_2000\_problem

. It therefore represents years from 0000 to 9999, year 0000 being equal to 1 BC and all others AD, similar to astronomical year numbering. However, years before 1583 (the first full year following the introduction of the Gregorian calendar) are not automatically allowed by the standard. Instead, the standard states that "values in the range \[0000\] through \[1582\] shall only be used by mutual agreement of the partners in information interchange".

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-24

To represent years before 0000 or after 9999, the standard also permits the expansion of the year representation but only by prior agreement between the sender and the receiver.

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-25

An expanded year representation \[±YYYYY\] must have an agreed-upon number of extra year digits beyond the four-digit minimum, and it must be prefixed with a + or - sign

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-26

instead of the more common AD/BC (or CE/BCE) notation; by convention 1 BC is labelled +0000, 2 BC is labeled -0001, and so on.

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-27

Calendar dates

http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISO\_8601&action=edit&section=5

#### Only allowed in the (now superseded) 2000 version:

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-datewithoutyear-29

Calendar date representations are in the form shown in the adjacent box. \[YYYY\] indicates a four-digit year, 0000 through 9999. \[MM\] indicates a two-digit month of the year, 01 through 12. \[DD\] indicates a two-digit day of that month, 01 through 31. For example, "5 April 1981" may be represented as either "1981-04-05"

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-isofaqtnd-19

extended format

or "19810405" in the

basic format

The standard also allows for calendar dates to be written with reduced precision. For example, one may write "1981-04" to mean "1981 April". One may simply write "1981" to refer to that year, "198" to refer to the

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decade

from 1980 to 1989 inclusive, or "19" to refer to the

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Century

from 1900 to 1999 inclusive. Although the standard allows both the "YYYY-MM-DD" and YYYYMMDD formats for complete calendar date representations, if the day \[DD\] is omitted then only the YYYY-MM format is allowed. By disallowing dates of the form YYYYMM, the standard avoids confusion with the

truncated representation

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#Truncated\_representations

IETF RFC 3339

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-55

defines a profile of ISO 8601 for use in

Internet protocols

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet\_protocol\_suite

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet\_standard

. It explicitly excludes durations and dates before the

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common\_era

. The more complex formats such as week numbers and ordinal days are not permitted.

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-56

RFC 3339 deviates from ISO 8601 in allowing a zero time zone offset to be specified as "-00:00", which ISO 8601 forbids. RFC 3339 intends "-00:00" to carry the connotation that it is not stating a preferred time zone, whereas the conforming "+00:00" or any non-zero offset connotes that the offset being used is preferred. This convention regarding "-00:00" is derived from earlier RFCs, such as RFC 2822 which uses it for timestamps in

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Email

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-57

RFC 2822 made no claim that any part of its timestamp format conforms to ISO 8601, and so was free to use this convention without conflict.

Building upon the foundations of RFC 3339, the

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet\_Engineering\_Task\_Force

introduced the Internet Extended Date/Time Format (IXDTF) in RFC 9557.

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO\_8601#cite\_note-58

This format extends the timestamp representation to include additional information such as an associated time zone name. The inclusion of time zone names is particularly useful for applications that need to account for events like daylight saving time transitions. Furthermore, IXDTF maintains compatibility with pre-existing syntax for attaching time zone names to timestamps, providing a standardized and flexible approach to timestamp representation on the internet. Example:

1996-12-19T16:39:57-08:00\[America/Los\_Angeles\]
