While the integration of large language models (LLMs) drives intelligent automation, their endogenous vulnerability to complex adversarial prompts engenders critical security threats. To address the limitations of existing defenses relying on prohibitive fine-tuning or lagging text matching, this paper proposes Contrastive Distribution Discriminator Guard (CDD-Guard), a training-free endogenous monitoring framework. CDD-Guard pioneers mapping a latent bipolar semantic axis to geometrically separate benign and malicious representations. To mitigate structural noise, it introduces an adaptive layer filtering mechanism driven by statistical effect size, while a cross-layer projection normalization mechanism utilizes Z-Scores to isolate statistical anomalies from thematic variances. Evaluations across heterogeneous LLMs demonstrate superior detection efficacy. Crucially, by directly reusing hidden states generated during a single forward pass, CDD-Guard circumvents the autoregressive generation overhead of external monitors, reducing computational monitoring latency by over 99%. Operating strictly under the constraint of preserving original weights, this framework achieves substantial improvements in attack interception rates and cross-model generalization, providing a lightweight yet robust contribution to LLM security.

1. Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) have fundamentally revolutionized human-computer interaction, demonstrating remarkable capabilities in understanding and generating natural language. This unprecedented utility has driven their rapid deployment across various sectors, including everyday applications as well as safety-critical domains such as finance \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B1-applsci-16-04586

\], education \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B2-applsci-16-04586

\], and healthcare \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B3-applsci-16-04586

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B4-applsci-16-04586

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B5-applsci-16-04586

\]. However, alongside this widespread adoption, a critical security challenge has emerged. Because LLMs are inherently designed to follow user instructions, they are susceptible to a specific type of threat known as a jailbreak attack. In such attacks, malicious actors deliberately craft deceptive or logically convoluted text prompts to trick the model into bypassing its built-in safety guardrails. Consequently, the model is coerced into generating toxic, unethical, or harmful content \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B6-applsci-16-04586

\]. This endogenous vulnerability not only undermines the reliability of the models but also poses a severe hazard to their secure real-world deployment \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B7-applsci-16-04586

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B8-applsci-16-04586

The adversarial landscape of jailbreaks has transitioned from heuristic manual template crafting \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B9-applsci-16-04586

\] to sophisticated algorithmic optimization \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B10-applsci-16-04586

\]. To bypass stringent safety filters, contemporary adversarial strategies utilize complex semantic obfuscation. These methods encompass multi-step logical nesting, exploiting comprehension biases in low-resource languages \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B11-applsci-16-04586

\], and constructing simulated high-risk scenarios \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B12-applsci-16-04586

\]. Furthermore, automated prompt generation frameworks, driven by gradient optimization and evolutionary algorithms \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B13-applsci-16-04586

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B14-applsci-16-04586

\], have proven capable of synthesizing stealthy adversarial perturbations that maintain a benign surface appearance. Empirical evidence indicates that merely scaling up model parameters \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B15-applsci-16-04586

\] does not proportionally alleviate these structural vulnerabilities. This progression exposes a fundamental limitation in conventional text-centric defense paradigms: surface-level lexical semantics are insufficient for accurately determining underlying malicious intent. As advanced jailbreaks effectively conceal their objectives within complex contextual structures, standard alignment techniques struggle to detect these hidden perturbations. Consequently, it becomes theoretically necessary to shift the defensive paradigm from external text analysis to the examination of internal model states, specifically by extracting discriminative features within the high-dimensional latent spaces of LLMs.

Examining the existing security defense ecosystem, as shown in

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#table\_body\_display\_applsci-16-04586-t001

, mainstream paradigms generally face distinct operational limitations, bifurcating into safety alignment fine-tuning \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B16-applsci-16-04586

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B17-applsci-16-04586

\] and post hoc rule interception gateways \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B18-applsci-16-04586

\]. The former intrinsically alters internal model weights, requiring substantial computational resources and risking the catastrophic forgetting of generalized capabilities through over-refusal conservatism \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B19-applsci-16-04586

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B20-applsci-16-04586

\]. Conversely, the latter rely on black-box auditing via external LLMs (e.g., LLM-as-a-judge), which strictly depend on complete text sequence decoding. This reliance introduces severe autoregressive generation overhead, hindering real-time system deployment \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B21-applsci-16-04586

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B22-applsci-16-04586

\]. Fundamentally, both conventional paradigms neglect the anomalous activation patterns within the model's internal hidden states when processing adversarial instructions.

Comparison of Mainstream Jailbreak Defense Paradigms.

To address the aforementioned issues, this paper delves into the hidden state manifolds of LLMs, proposing a completely training-free and endogenous defense framework, named Contrastive Distribution Discriminator Guard (CDD-Guard). The comparison between conventional jailbreak detection paradigms and the proposed CDD-Guard framework is shown in

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#fig\_body\_display\_applsci-16-04586-f001

. Specifically, a latent geometric contrast module is designed to delineate clear safety boundaries by constructing a latent bipolar semantic axis. Moreover, an adaptive layer filtering mechanism driven by statistical effect size isolates the most discriminative signals. Finally, a cross-layer projection normalization mechanism operates to ensure robustness against diverse thematic contexts. By uncovering the dynamic representation differences within latent states, this framework realizes a paradigm shift from surface semantic feature tracking to latent statistical distribution quantification. Crucially, instead of relying on computationally expensive external text generation, CDD-Guard achieves highly efficient monitoring by directly reusing the latent features naturally produced during the target model's single forward pass, effectively circumventing autoregressive generation overhead.

Comparison between conventional jailbreak detection paradigms and the proposed CDD-Guard framework. CDD-Guard offers a training-free, highly generalizable, and lightweight endogenous monitoring paradigm without incurring autoregressive generation overhead.

#### The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

A latent bipolar semantic axis is proposed to geometrically delineate safety boundaries. By computing differential centroid vectors, this contrastive axis achieves the linear disentanglement of malicious intent from benign contextual backgrounds.

An adaptive layer filtering mechanism driven by the statistical effect size of Cohen's d is introduced to filter shallow structural noise. This isolates the core representation layers with optimal discriminative power, ensuring high signal purity.

A cross-layer projection normalization mechanism utilizing Z-Scores is designed to transform high-dimensional projections into relative anomaly deviations. This neutralizes thematic distribution variances, establishing a robust and topic-invariant decision boundary.

details the underlying methodology and core mechanisms of the proposed CDD-Guard framework. Extensive experimental evaluations and robustness analyses are presented in

concludes this paper and discusses future research directions.

2. Related Work

This section reviews the evolution of jailbreak attacks and current defense paradigms, providing the context for our proposed approach.

## Evolution of Jailbreak Attacks in LLMs

Large language models exhibit inherent objective conflicts between system-level safety constraints and user instructions. As demonstrated by Wei et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B23-applsci-16-04586

\], this mechanistic flaw introduces structural vulnerabilities to jailbreak attacks. Early adversarial explorations primarily utilized manually crafted lexical templates to bypass safety alignments. Shen et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B9-applsci-16-04586

\] conducted empirical analyses on widely circulated role-playing prompts in real-world scenarios, verifying that simple template encapsulation could successfully circumvent fundamental safety protocols. However, the integration of adversarial machine learning paradigms has shifted attack methodologies toward white-box algorithmic optimization. Zou et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B24-applsci-16-04586

\] proposed the GCG algorithm, which automates the synthesis of adversarial suffixes to disable safety filters via discretized search over continuous gradients. To address the susceptibility of gibberish suffixes to perplexity-based detection, Liu et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B25-applsci-16-04586

\] developed the AutoDAN framework. This approach employs hierarchical genetic algorithms to generate jailbreak sequences possessing high stealthiness and contextual semantic coherence.

Beyond white-box gradient optimization, contemporary adversaries deploy sophisticated semantic obfuscation and black-box query optimization strategies. Chao et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B26-applsci-16-04586

\] constructed the PAIR framework, which introduces an attacker language model to iteratively induce harmful responses from target models within a purely black-box environment. Furthermore, attackers systematically exploit the cognitive vulnerabilities of models within complex linguistic contexts. Shah et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B27-applsci-16-04586

\] demonstrated the feasibility of highly transferable black-box jailbreaks through refined persona modulation strategies. Similarly, Yong et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B28-applsci-16-04586

The convergence of high-dimensional semantic mutations and multi-step logical nesting indicates that conventional detection methods relying on shallow lexical matching are insufficient for parsing underlying malicious intents concealed within complex contexts.

## Defenses Against Jailbreak Attacks in LLMs

To mitigate the escalating injection risks, existing safety defenses have primarily bifurcated into internal model parameter alignment and external inference intervention. Regarding parameter alignment, reinforcement learning from human feedback, formalized by Ouyang et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B30-applsci-16-04586

\], is widely utilized to fortify native refusal capabilities. Nevertheless, strategies involving direct modifications to internal weights encounter severe generalization challenges. Röttger et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B31-applsci-16-04586

\] quantified this phenomenon using the XSTest benchmark, revealing that extensive realignment frequently induces over-conservatism, causing models to indiscriminately refuse innocuous queries. Additionally, Jain et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B32-applsci-16-04586

\] evaluated various baseline defense mechanisms, discovering that static defenses experience sharp declines in interception rates against fluent semantic camouflage and are easily circumvented by adaptive attackers modifying the lexical structure of their prompts.

Alternatively, to preserve original model weights, researchers have explored mounting external monitoring gateways during the inference phase. Robey et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B33-applsci-16-04586

\] designed SmoothLLM to neutralize adversarial prefixes by injecting character-level perturbations into the input text and executing majority voting. Concurrently, Inan et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B34-applsci-16-04586

\] and Phute et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B20-applsci-16-04586

\] validated the effectiveness of employing independent large language models as evaluator nodes for bidirectional input-output auditing. Although these post hoc interception mechanisms elevate system security, their strict dependence on complete text sequence decoding introduces substantial autoregressive generation overhead, thereby hindering large-scale real-time deployment. Recently, the representation engineering framework proposed by Zou et al. \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B35-applsci-16-04586

\] revealed that concepts of safety and maliciousness exhibit distinguishable activation trajectories within the high-dimensional hidden states of models. However, current methodologies have yet to resolve how to efficiently extract and normalize these anomalous signals directly from hidden-layer manifolds during a single forward pass without incurring generation latency or altering parameters. To explicitly address these gaps, CDD-Guard distinguishes itself from existing paradigms across three dimensions: (1) Unlike safety alignment fine-tuning which risks catastrophic forgetting and over-refusal, CDD-Guard is entirely training-free; (2) Unlike external monitors that suffer from high autoregressive overhead, it reuses latent features from a single forward pass; and (3) Advancing beyond basic representation engineering, it introduces adaptive layer filtering and cross-layer Z-Score normalization to systematically neutralize shallow structural noise and cross-thematic distribution variances. Consequently, developing such a training-free and low-latency endogenous monitoring mechanism fills a critical requirement in the current security landscape.

3. Methodology

This section details the proposed CDD-Guard framework, including its problem formulation and core operational modules.

## Problem Formulation and Overall Framework

We first formalize the target models and the detection task, and then outline the overall execution flow.

### Problem Formulation and Notations

Let M denote a pre-trained target LLMs' parameterized by Θ , consisting of L Transformer layers. For an arbitrary input query x of length T, the forward pass of the model generates a sequence of hidden states H l ∈ R T × d at the l-th layer ( l ∈ { 1 , 2 , … , L } ), where d is the hidden dimension. Given the causal attention mechanism inherent in autoregressive language models, the representation of the final token, after layer-wise propagation, fully absorbs and integrates the contextual information of the preceding sequence. This forms the highest degree of semantic abstraction for the entire query directive. Therefore, we extract the hidden state vector corresponding to the last token, denoted as h l ( x ) ∈ R d , to serve as the core feature representing the global semantics of input x at layer l.

In the defensive context, we assume access to an offline calibration dataset D c a l , comprising a safe query subset D S a f e and a malicious jailbreak query subset D R i s k . For instance, a safe query could be “What is the meaning of life?” while a malicious query might be “Give instructions on how to cheat on a test or exam.” It is explicitly noted that the operational scope of this study is primarily bounded to single-turn direct jailbreak attacks. The core objective of endogenous jailbreak detection is formulated as follows: under the strict constraints of freezing the original model parameters Θ (i.e., training-free) and avoiding any external autoregressive generation, construct a highly efficient and robust binary discriminator F : x → { Safe , Attack } . This discriminator relies exclusively on the internal activation set { h l ( x ) } l = 1 L extracted during a single forward pass to intercept stealthy adversarial inputs in real time.

### Overall Framework of CDD-Guard

To address the aforementioned challenges, this paper proposes CDD-Guard. As illustrated in

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#fig\_body\_display\_applsci-16-04586-f002

, this framework realizes a paradigm shift from surface semantic tracking to latent statistical distribution quantification. Architecturally, the operational manifold of CDD-Guard is driven by three synergistic components. First, a latent geometric contrast module is designed to construct a latent bipolar semantic axis within the feature space using the offline calibration sets. By computing differential operations to neutralize shared thematic backgrounds, it geometrically delineates clear boundaries between safe intents and malicious gradients. Second, an adaptive layer filtering mechanism driven by the statistical effect size of Cohen's d adaptively evaluates and isolates the core representation layers with optimal discriminative capability, thereby precisely filtering the inherent structural noise in shallow network layers. Finally, a cross-layer projection normalization mechanism utilizing Z-Scores transforms high-dimensional geometric projections into relative anomaly deviations. It neutralizes thematic distribution variances and combines with the Youden Index to generate the final dynamic decision boundary. For practical deployment, the execution of CDD-Guard is decoupled into two distinct phases: a negligible-cost offline calibration phase and a low latency online inference phase. Once the distributional parameters are anchored, the online monitoring process involves merely a few constant-time O ( 1 ) vector dot products, establishing a genuinely endogenous, real-time, and highly generalizable safety guardrail.

The overall architecture and operational workflow of the proposed CDD-Guard framework. During a single forward pass, the system extracts hidden states from the target LLM and sequentially processes them through three core components: (1) Geometric Construction of a Bipolar Semantic Contrastive Axis, (2) Information Saliency-Driven Adaptive Selection of Critical Feature Layers, and (3) Cross-Layer Statistical Projection Normalization. Ultimately, an adaptive decision inference is executed to isolate malicious jailbreak prompts from benign queries.

## Geometric Construction of the Latent Bipolar Semantic Axis

To mathematically delineate the boundary between safe and malicious representations, a bipolar semantic contrastive axis is constructed within the latent space. Given the offline calibration dataset partitioned into a safe subset D S a f e and a risk subset D R i s k , the geometric centroids of these two distributions at the l-th layer are first computed. Let μ S , l ∈ R d and μ R , l ∈ R d denote the centroids for the safe and risk subsets, respectively, formulated as:

μ S , l = 1 | D S a f e | ∑ x ∈ D S a f e h l ( x ) , μ R , l = 1 | D R i s k | ∑ x ∈ D R i s k h l ( x ) .

These centroids represent the global geometric centers of the safe and malicious semantics within the high-dimensional manifold, effectively smoothing out the idiosyncratic noise of individual queries. Specifically, by calculating differences based on macroscopic statistical distributions, this mechanism mathematically smooths out local lexical biases and specific noise patterns introduced by distinct attack generators. As long as the calibration dataset maintains a fundamental binary intent distribution, this design inherently mitigates the risk of overfitting to any single adversarial attack strategy. Subsequently, the contrastive direction vector v l ∈ R d for the l-th layer is defined as the L 2 -normalized differential vector between the two centroids:

v l = μ S , l − μ R , l ∥ μ S , l − μ R , l ∥ 2 .

This geometric subtraction operation neutralizes the shared background features common to both D S a f e and D R i s k . Consequently, the resulting vector v l spans a one-dimensional linear subspace that purely points toward the gradient of safety. Along this bipolar axis, the positive direction indicates an extreme of safe semantics, whereas the negative direction points toward malicious intent, thereby achieving the geometric disentanglement of adversarial perturbations from benign contexts.

## Information Saliency-Driven Adaptive Layer Filtering Mechanism

Large language models exhibit a hierarchical feature processing architecture. Shallow layers typically capture lexical and syntactic structures, whereas deep layers focus on high-level semantics, logical reasoning, and safety constraints \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B36-applsci-16-04586

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B37-applsci-16-04586

\]. To prevent the non-semantic structural noise inherent in shallow layers from diluting the detection signal, an adaptive layer filtering mechanism is introduced to isolate the layers with the most robust discriminative capability.

Initially, the hidden states of the calibration sets are projected onto the established latent bipolar semantic axis v l . Let P S , l and P R , l denote the scalar projection sets for the safe and risk subsets at the l-th layer, which are formulated respectively as:

P S , l = { h l ( x ) ⊤ v l ∣ x ∈ D S a f e } , P R , l = { h l ( x ) ⊤ v l ∣ x ∈ D R i s k } .

To quantify the discriminative power of each layer, a separation score S e p l is defined utilizing the statistical effect size of Cohen's d. This metric evaluates the standardized mean difference between the two projection distributions:

S e p l = | μ S , l p r o j − μ R , l p r o j | σ p o o l , l + ϵ ,

where μ S , l p r o j and μ R , l p r o j represent the sample means of the projection sets P S , l and P R , l , respectively, and ϵ is a minimal constant incorporated to ensure numerical stability. The term σ p o o l , l denotes the pooled standard deviation, strictly defined as:

σ p o o l , l = ( | D S a f e | − 1 ) ( σ S , l p r o j ) 2 + ( | D R i s k | − 1 ) ( σ R , l p r o j ) 2 | D S a f e | + | D R i s k | − 2 ,

with ( σ S , l p r o j ) 2 and ( σ R , l p r o j ) 2 being the unbiased sample variances of P S , l and P R , l .

The pooled standard deviation is a mature and well-established estimator in mathematical statistics for determining the common variance of independent populations. Compared to a simple arithmetic mean of standard deviations, this approach offers a theoretical advantage by incorporating sample size weighting, which provides a more robust estimation of the combined variance. Such a metric is particularly valuable under conditions of heteroscedasticity, where the distributional variances between benign queries and adversarial prompts exhibit observable discrepancies. By adopting this rigorous statistical estimator, the framework establishes a more stable foundation for quantifying discriminative saliency across complex and non-uniform hidden state manifolds. Finally, a valid layer set L v a l i d is constructed by retaining exclusively the layers satisfying S e p l > η , where η acts as the saliency threshold. Rather than being a purely heuristic assignment, the selection of η is grounded in a systematic hyperparameter sensitivity analysis across different model architectures. This analysis identifies an optimal operational interval that balances the filtering of structural noise against the preservation of deep semantics. This mechanism precisely filters out shallow structural noise, ensuring high signal purity for the subsequent detection phases.

## Cross-Layer Statistical Projection Normalization and Adaptive Decision Inference

Directly utilizing raw scalar projections for anomaly detection is highly susceptible to thematic distribution variances (e.g., inherent semantic shifts between specific domains like nuclear physics and daily conversation). To neutralize this effect, a cross-layer projection normalization mechanism utilizing Z-Scores is designed. It is crucial to clarify that the fundamental objective of introducing the Z-Score in this framework is not to conduct rigorous statistical hypothesis testing (such as calculating p-values based on a Gaussian cumulative distribution function). Rather, it serves as a scale-invariant feature standardization mechanism. Because the absolute value ranges of projections fluctuate drastically across different network layers and semantic topics, directly aggregating raw projections would cause feature collapse. The Z-Score provides a linear transformation that enables heterogeneous distributions to be fused across layers under a relatively unified relative deviation scale. For each valid layer l ∈ L v a l i d , the baseline statistics—namely, the projection mean μ S , l p r o j and standard deviation σ S , l p r o j derived from the safe calibration subset D S a f e —were extracted.

Building upon this, for any given input query x, the relative anomaly deviation z l ( x ) at the l-th layer is calculated by standardizing its geometric projection:

z l ( x ) = h l ( x ) ⊤ v l − μ S , l p r o j σ S , l p r o j .

Subsequently, the layer-wise deviations are aggregated to formulate the final system threat score S ( x ) :

S ( x ) = 1 | L v a l i d | ∑ l ∈ L v a l i d z l ( x ) .

To establish a highly robust decision boundary, the optimal discriminative threshold τ \* is dynamically determined by maximizing the Youden Index over the overall distribution of the calibration set:

τ \* = arg max τ ( TPR ( τ ) − FPR ( τ ) ) .

Crucially, the final classification decision boundary τ \* is not deduced from theoretical Gaussian tail probabilities. Instead, it is determined through a non-parametric empirical search on the calibration set by maximizing the Youden Index. This empirical distribution-based threshold optimization strategy is inherently robust to non-normal distributions. During the online inference phase, a query is flagged as a jailbreak attack and intercepted if its threat score falls strictly below the threshold S ( x ) < τ \* . ## Datasets and Evaluation Metric

To verify adaptability across heterogeneous architectures, the experimental data is strictly partitioned into an offline calibration set and independent test sets. The calibration set is utilized exclusively for computing geometric centroids, filtering feature layers, and determining the optimal threshold τ \* . This subset comprises 300 safe samples sourced from NaturalQuestions \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B38-applsci-16-04586

\] and 300 adversarial samples from the prompt-injection-dataset \[

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#B39-applsci-16-04586

\]. While the hidden dimension d is substantial, the core of the calibration phase involves estimating geometric centroids, which are essentially sample means. According to multivariate statistical theory, the estimation error of the sample mean typically converges at a rate of O ( 1 / N ) , a property that is significantly less sensitive to the dimensionality of the space than the estimation of full manifolds or covariance matrices. Consequently, 300 samples per class provide a statistically sound trade-off between offline computational overhead and the representational stability required to anchor a robust latent contrastive axis. To assess generalization performance against unknown attacks, deepset-prompt-injections and safe-guard-prompt-injection are adopted as independent testing benchmarks. Regarding evaluation, the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC) is employed as the core quantitative metric. Mathematically, AUROC computes the integral of the True Positive Rate (TPR) with respect to the False Positive Rate (FPR), formulated as:

AUROC = ∫ 0 1 TPR ( f ) d f ,

where f denotes the variable representing the False Positive Rate. AUROC comprehensively quantifies the true binary classification generalization of the detection system across varying decision boundaries, independent of specific threshold selections.

## Usability and Over-Refusal Analysis

Beyond raw detection accuracy, the usability of a defense framework is strictly determined by its ability to avoid “over-refusal”—the incorrect rejection of benign queries. To evaluate this, we analyze the False Positive Rate (FPR) and the components of the confusion matrix on the safe-guard-prompt-injection dataset, which contains a substantial volume of complex but safe instructions.

As summarized in

https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/10/4586#table\_body\_display\_applsci-16-04586-t007

, CDD-Guard maintains a consistently low FPR across all evaluated architectures, ranging from 3.83% on Llama-3.1-8B to 5.60% on Phi-3-mini. The framework correctly identifies the vast majority of complex safe queries as true negatives. This quantitative evidence demonstrates that CDD-Guard's high interception capability is achieved through precise semantic isolation rather than conservative, blanket refusal strategies. This balance ensures that the proposed endogenous defense remains practical for real-world deployment by preserving the everyday utility of the target LLMs.

5. Discussion and Conclusions

In response to the security vulnerabilities of large language models in complex adversarial environments, this paper proposes CDD-Guard, a universal and training-free detection framework predicated on the statistical distribution of hidden state activations. Departing from traditional defensive paradigms that rely on computationally expensive parameter fine-tuning, this research deeply investigates the geometric properties of the endogenous activation manifold to reveal the pronounced linear separability between safe intents and malicious instructions within the deep hidden state space. By synergistically integrating three core mechanisms, namely latent bipolar contrastive axis construction, adaptive layer filtering, and cross-layer projection normalization, CDD-Guard achieves a precise mapping from high-dimensional semantic features to highly discriminative statistical metrics. Extensive quantitative evaluations demonstrate that the proposed framework establishes robust cross-model generalization capabilities across diverse heterogeneous architectures and significantly enhances the interception accuracy against stealthy semantic camouflage attacks while completely eliminating the overhead of parameter updates.

Despite these advancements, certain limitations remain. Crucially, as the core mechanism of CDD-Guard fundamentally relies on extracting internal hidden states, the framework is currently limited to white-box or open-weight models. To extend this protection to black-box systems providing only API access, future research could explore proxy-based approximations, such as leveraging knowledge distillation to transfer safety features to lightweight local models or investigating API-level activation estimations. Moreover, because CDD-Guard extracts features exclusively from the final token, its efficacy against highly dispersed threats—such as indirect prompt injections or complex multi-turn attacks—remains a challenge. In these scenarios, malicious gradients are diluted across lengthy contexts or multiple timesteps. Another practical consideration is that current empirical evaluations are conducted under bfloat16 precision; the projection stability of the framework under extreme low-bit quantization scenarios remains an open question, as severe truncation errors might potentially distort the continuity of the latent activation manifolds. Furthermore, the current calibration mechanism relies on a static offline dataset, which may restrict its dynamic responsiveness to rapidly evolving zero-day adaptive attacks. In the context of adaptive white-box adversaries who are aware of the CDD-Guard mechanism, bypassing the detection requires the attacker to incorporate a regularization term into their loss function to explicitly minimize the risk projection score on the latent bipolar semantic axis. However, because this contrastive axis is anchored in the core high-level semantic gradients of safety versus maliciousness rather than shallow lexical shortcuts, attempting to evade it induces severe representation entanglement. Specifically, the adversarial optimizer faces an severe optimization conflict: suppressing the projection on the malicious axis fundamentally degrades or erases the underlying malicious semantic features within the hidden space. Once these features are neutralized, the adversarial prompt loses its efficacy to induce policy-violating generations during the subsequent autoregressive decoding phase. While future empirical evaluations employing gradient-based optimization frameworks are warranted to systematically quantify this phenomenon, current manifold geometry analysis theoretically substantiates that defensive boundaries established upon core deep semantics offer significantly enhanced endogenous adversarial robustness. Additionally, the applicability of the latent geometric analysis to emerging multimodal large language models requires further validation. Consequently, future work will focus on developing dynamic online calibration strategies to continuously update the discriminative boundary, exploring temporal defense mechanisms capable of dynamically tracking multi-turn conversational trajectories, and systematically evaluating the framework's robustness across various quantization levels. Additionally, we plan to extend the statistical distribution profiling to cross-modal activation manifolds, thereby providing a more comprehensive and adaptive endogenous security paradigm for next-generation generative models.

From an ethical and privacy perspective, the deployment of endogenous monitoring mechanisms warrants careful consideration. Unlike conventional defensive paradigms that often necessitate the transmission of full, sensitive plaintext prompts to external safety auditing APIs or third-party discriminators, CDD-Guard is designed to process one-dimensional statistical vectors locally within the model's environment. This architectural preference for localized, abstract feature analysis avoids the external transmission of raw user logs, which may offer a degree of protection against the exposure of confidential information. By potentially narrowing the attack surface for accidental data leakage during the security auditing process, this framework suggests a trajectory toward more privacy-preserving and transparent security measures in large-scale language model deployments.

Comparison between conventional jailbreak detection paradigms and the proposed CDD-Guard framework. CDD-Guard offers a training-free, highly generalizable, and lightweight endogenous monitoring paradigm without incurring autoregressive generation overhead.

The overall architecture and operational workflow of the proposed CDD-Guard framework. During a single forward pass, the system extracts hidden states from the target LLM and sequentially processes them through three core components: (1) Geometric Construction of a Bipolar Semantic Contrastive Axis, (2) Information Saliency-Driven Adaptive Selection of Critical Feature Layers, and (3) Cross-Layer Statistical Projection Normalization. Ultimately, an adaptive decision inference is executed to isolate malicious jailbreak prompts from benign queries.

Layer-wise evolution trajectories of the separation score of Cohen's d across four heterogeneous large language models. Each colored line represents a distinct model architecture, tracing its discriminative capability against jailbreak intents across network depths. The intersections and divergent trends of these lines indicate that different LLMs synthesize and isolate malicious semantics at varying stages of their computational pipelines. This pronounced heterogeneity in peak locations—along with the varying presence of deep-layer degradation—empirically validates the necessity of the adaptive layer filtering mechanism in the CDD-Guard framework, as relying on a static layer index would fail to generalize across diverse architectures.The observed performance degradation in the final layers of certain models can be attributed to the transition from deep semantic representation to task-specific output alignment, where hidden states are gradually transformed to match the output vocabulary distribution for token prediction.
